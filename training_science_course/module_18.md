# Module 18: Feed the Machine — Why Under-Eating Is Destroying Your Dancing

## The Big Idea
Dancers chronically under-fuel by 20–30% of their actual energy requirements — and research documents exactly what happens as a result. Not vague fatigue. Not "less energy." A specific, cascading hormonal and metabolic disaster that includes suppressed bone density, reduced muscle mass, degraded neuromuscular coordination, and dramatically elevated injury risk. The cruel irony is that many dancers under-eat intentionally, for aesthetic reasons, and produce the exact opposite of the body and performance they're pursuing. Adequate fueling is not the enemy of your goals. It is the precondition for achieving them.

## Why Your Brain Works This Way

### Low Energy Availability: The Cascade Begins

**Energy availability (EA)** is defined as dietary energy intake minus exercise energy expenditure, expressed relative to fat-free mass. The IOC consensus statement on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) identifies a clear threshold: when EA falls below approximately **30 kcal per kilogram of fat-free mass per day**, the body enters a state of energy conservation that selectively compromises systems it judges non-essential for immediate survival.

The hormonal cascade begins within days of sustained under-fueling:

- **Triiodothyronine (T3)** — the active thyroid hormone — decreases, suppressing metabolic rate. The body adapts to restriction by burning less.
- **Insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1)** drops, reducing protein synthesis and tissue repair capacity.
- **Luteinizing hormone (LH) pulsatility** becomes disrupted, suppressing sex hormone production — testosterone in males, estrogen in females.
- **Cortisol** remains elevated — the catabolic, tissue-breaking-down hormone that's supposed to be temporary — while anabolic hormones decline.

The net result is a body in a state of accelerated breakdown with reduced repair capacity. You are practicing harder and recovering less with every week this continues.

### The Bone Density Trap

Nattiv and colleagues' (2007) work on the Female Athlete Triad — later expanded into the RED-S framework — established one of the most striking findings in sports medicine for dancers: **bones in dancers with restricted dietary intake show below-average mineral density despite the fact that daily practice should be producing above-average density.**

Estrogen is the primary anabolic hormone regulating bone mineral density in female dancers. LEA suppresses estrogen via LH pulsatility disruption. Simultaneously, elevated cortisol actively promotes bone resorption. The mechanical loading stimulus from practice — which would normally stimulate bone strengthening — is overridden by the hormonal environment created by under-fueling. The result is bones that are more stressed but less capable of adaptive remodeling. Stress fractures become not a possibility but an eventuality.

### Glycogen: The Actual Fuel for Salsa

Salsa's plyometric, rotational, and explosive demands are **glycogen-dependent** (see Module 15 on the ATP-PC and lactic energy systems). Glycogen — glucose stored in muscle and liver — is the preferred fuel for high-intensity anaerobic work. A single intensive salsa session can deplete **30–50% of muscle glycogen stores**.

Dancers training with chronically depleted glycogen experience: premature fatigue, reduced explosive power output, degraded neuromuscular coordination (the "brain fog" of glycogen depletion), and increased cortisol as the body turns to gluconeogenesis (breaking down muscle protein for fuel). Burke and colleagues' work on carbohydrate periodization establishes that timing carbohydrate intake around training — not merely achieving an adequate daily total — optimizes glycogen availability for high-intensity sessions.

### Protein Distribution: When You Eat Matters

Beyond total protein intake, the *distribution* of protein across meals significantly affects muscle protein synthesis rates. Research on protein timing (Moore et al.; Phillips and Van Loon) establishes that muscle protein synthesis is maximized when protein intake is distributed across **4–5 meals at 20–30 g per meal**, rather than concentrated in one or two large doses.

Dancers who eat light throughout the day and load up on protein at dinner are not optimizing the anabolic stimulus from their dietary protein. The muscle protein synthesis response has both a ceiling per meal and a refractory period — spread it out, or leave gains on the table.

## What You're Going to Do About It

Calculate your actual daily energy expenditure (TDEE) using a validated activity-adjusted formula, adding a specific estimate for dance training hours. Ensure dietary energy intake meets or exceeds this number.

Structure **carbohydrate-dense meals** (rice, oats, sweet potato, fruit) within 2 hours before and within 1 hour after intensive training sessions. Distribute **20–30 g of high-quality protein** (eggs, Greek yogurt, lean meat, legumes) across 4–5 daily meals.

If you're not hungry after training, prioritize liquid nutrition — a smoothie or milk — to initiate glycogen resynthesis and muscle protein synthesis when appetite is suppressed. Not eating after a hard session is not discipline. It is a hormonal miscalculation.

## The Mind-Blowing Part

**Dancers chronically under-fuel by 20–30% — and often do so intentionally.**

The RED-S literature documents the direct consequences: lower bone density, suppressed metabolic rate, reduced muscle mass, impaired immune function, and substantially higher injury risk. The cruelest part of this picture is that under-fueling for aesthetic reasons produces the opposite of the desired result. Muscle loss replaces fat loss. Bones break more easily. Performance declines. The hormonal environment suppresses the very tissue remodeling that training is supposed to produce.

Adequate fueling is not in conflict with body composition goals. It is the precondition for achieving them sustainably — and it is the precondition for the training adaptations described in every other module in this course to actually occur. None of the other seventeen tools work properly on an underfueled system.

## Knowledge Check

**1. What is the energy availability threshold below which the body enters metabolic conservation and hormonal suppression, according to RED-S research?**
A) Less than 50 kcal/kg of fat-free mass per day
B) Less than 30 kcal/kg of fat-free mass per day
C) Less than 15 kcal/kg of fat-free mass per day
D) Less than 10 kcal/kg of fat-free mass per day
*Correct Answer: B) The IOC RED-S consensus (Mountjoy et al., 2014) cites 30 kcal/kg of fat-free mass per day as the threshold below which hormonal disruption, metabolic rate suppression, and bone density compromise begin to manifest.*

**2. Why do dancers with chronically low energy availability show reduced bone mineral density despite performing high-impact daily training?**
A) The impact forces during dance are insufficient to stimulate bone remodeling
B) Calcium absorption is impaired by the high-sodium content of typical dance facility food
C) LEA-induced estrogen deficiency removes the hormonal signal for bone anabolic remodeling, while elevated cortisol promotes bone resorption — overriding the mechanical loading stimulus
D) Dance training preferentially loads the upper body, leaving lower-body bones understimulated
*Correct Answer: C) Estrogen is the primary anabolic regulator of bone mineral density in female athletes. LEA suppresses estrogen via LH pulsatility disruption while maintaining elevated catabolic cortisol, creating a net bone resorption environment that the mechanical loading stimulus cannot overcome.*

**3. What protein intake distribution pattern best maximizes muscle protein synthesis rates throughout the day?**
A) One large protein meal (60–80 g) in the evening after training
B) Two moderate protein meals (40 g each) at breakfast and dinner
C) Four to five meals each containing 20–30 g of protein, evenly distributed throughout the day
D) Protein intake timing does not matter; only total daily protein quantity affects muscle protein synthesis
*Correct Answer: C) Research on protein timing by Moore, Phillips, and Van Loon establishes that muscle protein synthesis rates are maximized by distributing 20–30 g of high-quality protein across 4–5 daily meals rather than concentrating intake, because the anabolic response to protein has both a ceiling per meal and a refractory period.*
