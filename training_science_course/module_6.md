# Module 6: The Speed Paradox, Go Faster by Making Yourself Smaller

## The Big Idea
When the music speeds up, every instinct in your body screams: move bigger, try harder, push faster. That instinct is wrong, and it's exactly why most dancers fall apart at high tempos. The biomechanical truth that elite sprinters and the fastest salsa dancers both know is this: **maximum speed requires you to actively shrink your movement**. Bigger is slower. Smaller is faster. And once you feel it, you can never go back.

## Why Your Brain Works This Way

Let's start with a physics equation that will change how you practice forever.

**Velocity = step frequency × step length.**

At any given level of effort, if you increase one of those variables, the other has to decrease. You cannot maximize both simultaneously. This isn't opinion, it's a mathematical constraint. And Weyand and colleagues (2000) proved through force-plate analysis of elite sprinters that the fastest humans on earth don't achieve top speed by taking the longest possible steps. They achieve it by maximizing **step frequency**, which requires accepting, and actively training, a reduction in step amplitude.

The reason is purely mechanical. A longer, wider step forces your center of mass to travel a greater horizontal distance over your stance foot before the next push-off can begin. More distance = more time. More time = lower frequency. At 180+ BPM in salsa, this becomes acutely limiting. Your feet simply cannot complete their full cycle in the time available if the steps are wide.

### The Hidden Cost of Bouncing

Cavanagh and Kram (1989) analyzed the energetics of locomotion and identified **vertical oscillation**, unnecessary up-and-down movement of your center of mass, as one of the primary sources of wasted energy during movement. Every centimeter you bounce upward during a weight transfer is kinetic energy diverted vertically, contributing nothing to horizontal movement or rotation.

Watch beginner and intermediate dancers at high tempos: they bounce. They rise onto their balls of their feet with an exaggerated lift. Each bounce adds height, which adds time in the air, which slows the cycle. Elite salsa dancers at 200 BPM look like they're barely moving, because their center of mass is traveling in a nearly horizontal plane. The steps are compact, precise, and directly beneath their hips. The visual impression is effortlessness, because no energy is being wasted on movement that doesn't serve the tempo.

### Feet Under Hips, The Geometry of Speed

When your foot lands close to your center of mass, directly beneath or slightly ahead, the ground reaction force vectors align favorably with your intended direction of movement. When the foot lands wide, lateral to your hip, the reaction force creates a braking component that must be overcome. You're fighting yourself with every step.

Research on elite sprinters shows that their performance correlates strongly with how quickly the foot is repositioned beneath the center of mass after each ground contact. In dance terms: compact, centered steps are not aesthetically timid. They are mechanically optimal. The dancer who looks most at home at 210 BPM isn't trying harder, they've trained the habit of making the movement smaller under pressure.

## What You're Going to Do About It

When BPM rises above your comfortable range, typically above 170 BPM for most intermediate dancers, implement a deliberate "shrink" protocol. Three specific things:

1. **Reduce step size by approximately 30–40%.** Yes, that much.
2. **Keep both feet within hip-width distance.** Not wider.
3. **Eliminate any intentional vertical lift from weight transfers.** Think horizontal disc, not trampoline.

Drill this compact footwork pattern at 180, 190, and 200 BPM before attempting any stylistic additions. Get comfortable being small before you try to be fancy.

## The Mind-Blowing Part

Elite sprinters must actively reduce their amplitude by up to **6.10%** to maintain maximum velocity. That reduction is not accidental, it's deliberate and trained. Every elite sprinter knows that trying to open up their stride at top speed makes them slower.

Applied to dance: the performers who look most effortless at blistering tempos have trained the habit of consciously making their movement smaller under pressure. This runs directly against the natural instinct to try harder, move bigger, and force the tempo. The compact step is the secret ingredient. The next time the DJ speeds it up and you feel the urge to try harder, do the opposite. Get smaller. Get faster.

## Knowledge Check

**1. According to Weyand et al. (2000), how do elite sprinters primarily achieve maximum velocity?**
A) By maximizing step length
B) By maximizing vertical displacement for more powerful push-off
C) By maximizing step frequency, which requires managing amplitude reduction
D) By increasing arm swing amplitude to generate momentum
*Correct Answer: C) Force-plate analysis showed that elite speed comes from maximizing step frequency, which necessitates reducing step length beyond the optimal threshold, the two variables compete for the same time budget, and frequency wins.*

**2. What is the primary mechanical reason that wide, lateral steps limit dance speed at high tempos?**
A) Wide steps reduce ankle flexibility
B) Wide steps increase ground contact time by requiring the center of mass to travel further before push-off can initiate
C) Wide steps recruit slow-twitch fibers preferentially
D) Wide steps cause the vestibular system to send incorrect balance signals
*Correct Answer: B) When the foot lands wide, the center of mass must travel a greater horizontal distance over the stance foot before the next step can begin, directly increasing the time per cycle and limiting maximum frequency.*

**3. What does Cavanagh and Kram's (1989) research on locomotion energetics identify as a primary source of wasted energy during high-tempo movement?**
A) Excessive arm swing
B) Rapid breathing patterns
C) Vertical oscillation of the center of mass
D) High muscle activation in the upper body
*Correct Answer: C) Vertical displacement of the center of mass diverts kinetic energy upward rather than forward or rotational, contributing to increased ground contact time and reduced step frequency. Bouncing is the enemy of speed.*
